“Suspending Damage”

A shifting in education

Eve Tuck 2009 · 5 min. read

Exam-oriented education reflects what Eve Tuck terms a damage-centered logic — a way of seeing students primarily through their perceived deficiencies and failures. In this framework, learners are treated as problems to be corrected, rather than as individuals with strengths, agency, and potential. The focus is on fixing what is broken, rather than nurturing what is already present.

Tuck critiques damage-centered research for the way it “frames entire communities as depleted, ruined, and hopeless” (p. 409). This perspective strips people of complexity and resilience, reducing them to symptoms of harm. Similarly, exam-based education systems often frame underperforming students not as products of structural inequality or rigid curricula, but as personally lacking — in intelligence, effort, or discipline.

She warns: “All we are left with is the damage” (p. 415). This haunting conclusion mirrors how standardized testing can flatten the identities of students into mere numbers, erasing their histories, dreams, and creative capacities. The more students are evaluated solely on scores, the more their humanity is lost in the process.

In contrast, a desire-based approach to education resists this deficit narrative. It values “hope, visions, and the wisdom of lived lives” (p. 417), and seeks to center youth not as broken but as whole — as experts in their own experiences. This model of education invites students to co-create knowledge, to imagine liberatory futures, and to engage critically with the world around them.

By shifting from damage to desire, education can move beyond remediation and toward transformation — cultivating not just academic success, but dignity, purpose, and social change.
